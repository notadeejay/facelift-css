Part 1

Manage & Engage Stakeholders

Guiding Question

How can you better understand a stakeholder's point of view, so you can effectively manage and engage with them?

Motivators, Allegiances, and Losses

As a business analyst, your overall role focuses on noticing, uncovering, and addressing business process problems within a company. One aspect of this is understanding the people that interact with these processes — your company's stakeholders.

As you know from the previous module, you need to analyze your stakeholders to understand the best and most effective ways to interact with them and get the information you need to address business process problems. One methodology you can use for this type of analysis is a stakeholder perspective analysis.

When you disagree with stakeholders or your stakeholders hesitate to make decisions or take action, it may be unclear to you why they cannot understand your viewpoint and move forward. The following factors — motivators, allegiances, and losses — will help you understand what a stakeholder is thinking and what is impacting their stance during a conversation or interview.

  • Motivators are what is important to your stakeholder.
  • Allegiances are the people or groups your stakeholder is closely connected to.
  • Losses are what your stakeholder has to lose if an action is taken.

By looking at a stakeholder's motivators, allegiances, and losses, you can take inventory of their considerations and view things from their perspective. A stakeholder perspective analysis will ultimately help you communicate more effectively with stakeholders during interviews or follow-up conversations.

Case Study: Easy Textbooks

Imagine you are a business analyst at Easy Textbooks, a company that sells textbooks to schools and colleges. Your CEO met with you and asked you to analyze the process the company uses to track sales. The CEO wants you to suggest ways to make improvements, since there were inaccuracies in many of the recent sales reports. 

To gain more information about the sales process, you talk to one of your stakeholders, Jen the sales manager. Jen says the sales process does not need to change at all, and she is very frustrated that the business process analysis is happening. Jen expresses this to you in an email.

In order to respond effectively, you need to do a quick stakeholder perspective analysis to better understand Jen's point of view.

Jen is very organized and tends to prioritize order and consistency. In the past, she has resisted change at all costs. Jen reports to the CEO and is very tied to her team, the sales team. Right now, Jen has a lot of decision-making power and respect in the company, although this could change.

Part 2

A Framework for Conducting Stakeholder Interviews

Motivators, Allegiances, and Losses

Considering how many questions can be asked and how much information can be covered, interviews can get quite complex. For this reason, it is important to keep interviews as focused and purposeful as possible. In addition, these interviews can take place through multiple formats, including face-to-face, email and Slack. You will want to make sure you utilize best practices for business communication on each of these platforms and ask the right questions to satisfy your needs.

As a reminder, when conducting a business process analysis, your key goals typically span the following:

  • Understand the goals of the business process, both for the business and the people involved with/affected by the process
  • Develop a complete understanding of the as-is business process and document it.
  • Surface core pain points and potential issues or gaps with the existing process
  • Determine possible improvements for a to-be process.

Let's simplify these into four categories:

  • Business Process Goals
  • As-Is Process Steps
  • Pain Points & Issues
  • Potential Improvements

Take a look at the following table to understand each of these categories, their goals, and the types of questions to ask for each. 

Stakeholder Interview Analysis: Goals and Categories

Category Key Goal Questions You Might Ask Important Notes
Business Process Goals Identify the goals of the business process, both for the business and the people involved with/affected by the process.
  • What is the objective of this process?
  • What do you use this process for?
  • What is your role in this process?
Keep in mind that goals will sometimes vary across stakeholders, depending on their individual involvement with the business process. However, the overarching goal of the process for the business at large will likely remain the same.
As-Is Process Steps Develop a complete understanding of the as-is business process and document it.
  • What causes this process to begin? What is the first step in the process?
  • When do you know this process is finished? What is the ending point?
  • What are the steps involved in this process?
  • Why is the process done this way?
The process steps you hear might differ among different people you interview, especially when considering process steps that are external to Salesforce.
Pain Points & Issues Surface core pain points and potential issues or gaps with the existing process.
  • What is your #1 pain point with this process? 
    • What is your #1 pain point with this process? 
  • What would you say is the biggest area for improvement?
  • What is the #1 complaint partners (or customers) have?
You should independently investigate and validate pain points and potential issues that interviewees surface during interviews before regarding them as genuine issues. You should also keep the larger goal(s) of the business process in mind when considering which pain points are important to address.
Potential Improvements Determine possible improvements for a to-be process.
  1. If you could design the ideal process, what would that look like?
  2. How could this step be simplified?
    1. If you could design the ideal process, what would that look like?
    2. How could this step be simplified?
Suggestions from interviewees are not always the same as the actual best improvements to make to a process. Again, you should validate these yourself as a business analyst and keep the process goals in mind.

As you might have gathered from the “Important Notes” column, a helpful consideration to keep in mind when interviewing is that stakeholders' answers are subjective. This means that you may hear different answers from different people. For instance, the business process steps you hear from one stakeholder might be summarized differently by another. Orpain points and suggestions for improvement might differ among different types of stakeholders. Remember, each of your stakeholders has a unique perspective and role in the process at hand, and it is your job to form the most holistic and inclusive understanding that you can about the process in order to solve the most salient challenges.

In general, you should take answers you receive from stakeholders as areas for exploration in your overall journey to understand and improve a business process. This means that sometimes, you may need to interview certain individuals more than once or even cross-validate what you hear in interviews with other individuals and with data.
